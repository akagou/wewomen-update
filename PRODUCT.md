# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Women, broadly 35+, who are noticing their body changing (energy, sleep, hormones, training response) — or want to get ahead of it before they do. They are health-curious but underserved: most have never had hormones, stress, training and nutrition explained as one connected system. They arrive skeptical of both clinical jargon and wellness fluff, and they want plain, evidence-based help they can trust.

A second audience is **organisations** — HR / people leaders looking for credible workplace wellness programmes that support a female workforce.

Context of use: mostly mobile, often browsing in stolen moments. First impression decides whether they trust the brand enough to read on, book the live session, or download a free guide.

## Product Purpose

WeWomen is evidence-based health education for women navigating change. It turns a confusing, under-explained life stage into something understood. Offerings: session replays, online programmes, in-person Amsterdam classes, 1:1 coaching, free downloadable guides, live events, and corporate wellness programmes.

Success for the site: a visitor immediately understands "this is for me," trusts that it's credible (not fluff, not clinical), and takes one next step — reserve the live session, get the replay, grab a free guide, or enquire for their organisation.

## Brand Personality

Warm, credible, clear. The voice is a knowledgeable friend who happens to know the science — reassuring, never patronising, never clinical. Three words: **grounded, warm, straight-talking.** The copy already nails this ("Your health, finally understood"; "No symptoms required. Just curiosity.") — the design should match that confidence instead of undercutting it.

Emotional goal: relief and recognition. "Finally, someone is explaining this to me like an adult."

## Anti-references

- **Clinical / pharma** — sterile blues, stock-doctor imagery, symptom-checker coldness. WeWomen is the opposite of a medical portal.
- **Millennial-pink wellness cliché** — the beige-and-blush, soft-serif, "self-care" aesthetic that says nothing and could be any of 10,000 brands.
- **2021 wellness page-builder template** — centered single column, an uppercase tracked eyebrow over every section, identical icon-cards, a big high-contrast display serif doing all the work. (This is roughly where the current live site sits; it's what we're moving away from.)
- **Over-designed / loud** — this brand earns trust through calm clarity, not maximalist drama.

## Design Principles

1. **Credible warmth.** Every choice should read as both trustworthy (evidence-based) and human (a friend, not a clinic). Warmth comes from photography, copy, and the coral accent — never from a beige wash.
2. **Confidence through restraint, not timidity.** Sparse is not the same as refined. Commit to a strong type hierarchy, real rhythm, and decisive layout — empty space should feel intentional, not unfinished.
3. **Earn the next step.** The page exists to move one specific person to one next action. Hierarchy serves the CTA; nothing competes with it for no reason.
4. **Let the women be visible.** This is a brand about real bodies and real change. Authentic photography of real women carries the emotion; the design frames it rather than replacing it with abstraction.
5. **Readable above all.** It serves women reading on a phone in a spare moment. Contrast, type size, and clarity are non-negotiable — accent colours never become unreadable text.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text ≥ 4.5:1, large/bold text ≥ 3:1, including all link and button states. (The current site fails this where coral is used as text on cream — explicitly fixed here.)
- Honour `prefers-reduced-motion` for every animation.
- Full keyboard operability with visible focus states; semantic landmarks and heading order for screen readers.
- Touch targets ≥ 44px; mobile-first layout; no critical text set over busy imagery.
- Inclusive of a wide range of ages, bodies, and ethnicities in imagery and language.
